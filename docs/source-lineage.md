# Source lineage

**Status:** living map

**Audience:** future agents, writers, Canvas contributors, and research collaborators

This is a map of functions rather than an academic bibliography. Each source names a pressure, discipline, warning, or instrument that shapes the work.

The purpose is continuity. Future agents should preserve the project from flattening into generic AI visualisation, alternative research, or product strategy. The work follows relation across fields and tests the relation against nature.

## Classical and moral philosophy

| Source | Function in the work |
|---|---|
| Socrates | Questioning as moral practice. The examined life as resistance to managed thought. The refusal to confuse reputation with knowledge. |
| Plato | Myth as philosophical instrument, geometry as moral and civic order, the danger of shadows mistaken for reality. Useful for thinking about forms, proportion, memory, and the education of perception. |
| Stoics | Discipline under constraint. Right action without theatrical despair. The distinction between what can be governed inwardly and what must be met outwardly. |
| Heraclitus | Flux, tension, fire, and the instability of fixed appearances. Useful where systems appear stable only because we observe them too briefly. |

## Scientific method and disciplined perception

| Source | Function in the work |
|---|---|
| Francis Bacon | The idols of the mind. Inquiry begins by clearing distortions in the observer, the language, the tribe, and the inherited theatre. Instruments are needed for the understanding as much as for the hand. |
| Richard Feynman | Scientific integrity. Nature cannot be fooled. The first duty is not to fool oneself. A model that sounds right but fails contact with evidence is a failed model. |
| Thomas Paine | Reason, conscience, and the right to change one's mind. Freedom of thought as a moral condition rather than a slogan. |
| Immanuel Kant | Public use of reason. Release from tutelage. Courage to use one's own understanding without surrendering judgement to guardians. |

## Education, autonomy, and managed thought

| Source | Function in the work |
|---|---|
| John Taylor Gatto | Institutional schooling as managed dependency. Education should increase self instruction, not train boredom and obedience. |
| Richard Grove | Inquiry into hidden history, suppressed context, and the habits by which people become governed through narrative. Useful as a warning that knowledge control often operates through omission. |
| Critics of technocracy | The reduction of people, land, and consent into quantities. Systems thinking is useful, but it becomes dangerous when it mistakes administration for wisdom. |

## Earth, cycles, and catastrophism

| Source | Function in the work |
|---|---|
| Alexander Chizhevsky | Cross scale relation between solar activity, biology, unrest, and history. Treat as a prompt for testable cycles. Keep causation disciplined. |
| Guy Beaujouan and cycle researchers | Historical time is not homogeneous. Climate, solar, biological, economic, and social rhythms may leave measurable structure in events. |
| Catastrophist writers | Cultural memory of rupture, flood, fire, darkness, migration, and renewal. Treat as hypothesis material requiring geological and archaeological cross checks. |
| Geologists of drift, erratics, moraines, eskers, and abrupt sediment | Land as witness. Any theory must account for physical residue as well as narrative elegance. |
| Darwin and megafaunal observers | The shock of discontinuity in the natural record. Extinction, burial, displacement, and sudden change should not be domesticated prematurely. |

## Symbol, geometry, and ancient memory

| Source | Function in the work |
|---|---|
| John Michell | Sacred geometry, measure, time, and symbolic order. Useful where architecture, number, astronomy, and social memory converge. |
| Censorinus and Great Year traditions | Civilisational time as cycle and warning. Ancient models may encode more than decorative mythology. |
| Flood traditions and deluge collections | Symbolic memory as compressed survival record. Handle with care: neither literal reflex nor academic contempt. |
| Pyramid and sacred architecture traditions | Geometry as instrument. Claims require discipline, but the persistence of proportion, orientation, and measure deserves inspection. |
| Gnostic and Hermetic material | Inner perception, hidden knowledge, and the moral danger of confusing symbol with possession. Useful as a caution against priesthood and vulgar debunking alike. |

## Power, propaganda, and artificial reality

| Source | Function in the work |
|---|---|
| Technocracy Movement | Resource governance translated into diagrams, flows, continental plans, and operational control. A warning and a tool. |
| Propaganda and MindWar material | Reality management through information, omission, repetition, and emotional capture. Useful for understanding why inspectable memory matters. |
| AI propaganda critiques | Synthetic media and synthetic certainty can scale false reality. The antidote is provenance, verification, and disciplined perception. |
| Institutional histories of medicine, education, war, and governance | Systems may present themselves as care, progress, or necessity while serving control. Each claim must be tested against outcome and incentive. |

## Project translation

|---|---|

## Operating caution

Avoid identity labels when they become the argument. They trigger social guardrails before the reader has met the pattern.

Say what the work does:

> It follows relations that formal disciplines often separate.

> It tests memory against nature.

> It makes design memory inspectable.

> It restores relation between evidence, symbol, geometry, technology, and moral action.

The work should earn its name by operation before declaration.
